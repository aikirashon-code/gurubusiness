Hospital Notary Services in Trenton: A Caregiver's Guide

Navigating power of attorney, healthcare directives, and Signature by Mark pathways at Capital Health Regional Medical Center and surrounding Mercer County facilities.

A medical emergency can create an urgent need for legally witnessed documents that protect a patient's wishes and authorize trusted family members or caregivers to act on their behalf. Time matters enormously — and the stress of the situation makes navigating bureaucratic hurdles feel impossible.

1

Documents Most Commonly Needed at the Bedside

Durable Power of Attorney (Financial)

Authorizes a trusted individual to manage financial assets, bank accounts, property, and business affairs on behalf of the patient during incapacitation. Critical to establish before a patient loses legal capacity.

Healthcare Power of Attorney / Proxy Directive

Names a healthcare agent empowered to make medical decisions — including treatment acceptance, surgical consent, and end-of-life care choices — when the patient cannot communicate their own wishes.

Living Will / Advance Directive

Specifies the patient's direct wishes regarding life-sustaining treatment, resuscitation preferences, artificial nutrition, and palliative care — legally binding guidance for both medical teams and family members.

Last Will & Testament

Designates heirs, asset distribution, and guardian appointments. Must be executed while the testator has testamentary capacity — our notary assesses and documents this carefully at bedside.

The Three Legal Requirements for Bedside Notarization

Our trained notaries assess each of these requirements thoughtfully and compassionately before witnessing any bedside signature:

1

Verifiable Identity

A current, government-issued photo ID is the standard requirement. If the patient does not have identification available at the facility, New Jersey law permits identity verification through two Credible Witnesses — individuals who personally know the signer and can swear under oath to their identity. Our notary will guide this process.

2

Mental Capacity (Lucidity)

The signer must understand the nature and content of the document being executed, and must be acting of their own free will without undue influence or coercion. Our notary conducts a brief, respectful conversation to confirm lucidity — this is a legal protection for the patient, not an interrogation.

3

Physical Ability to Sign

Standard signature preferred. If illness, injury, or paralysis prevents a traditional handwritten signature, NJ law provides two alternatives: (1) Signature by Mark — any physical mark the patient can make, and (2) Signature by Direction — the notary signs at the patient's explicit oral direction in the presence of two disinterested witnesses.
